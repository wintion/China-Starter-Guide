## Quick Answer

If this is your first trip to China, start in this order: preparation, arrival logistics, payment, and transportation.  
If you only read three guides first, make them [How to Pay in China](/guides/payment/how-to-pay-in-china), [Is China Safe?](/guides/prepare/is-china-safe), and [From Airport to City in China](/guides/transport/airport-to-city).

## Start Here: Don’t Begin With Scattered Tips

First-time visitors to China usually do not suffer from a lack of information.  
They suffer from information arriving in the wrong order.

This page is a starting route, not a giant checklist.  
Use it to sort out what matters first, what can wait, and what will make the biggest difference to how calm the trip feels once you land.

---

## Step 1: Build a Basic Picture of How China Works

Before you go too deep, it helps to anchor a few realities:
- Many Chinese cities feel orderly and low-drama in daily life
- A lot of systems are app-driven and screen-driven
- Many processes are structured and procedural
- A lot of pre-trip anxiety comes from assumptions rather than on-the-ground experience

Getting this mental model right makes the rest of the preparation easier.

## Step 2: Sort Out Entry, Visa, and Documents Early

Before departure, make sure you understand:
- Whether you need a visa
- How long you can stay
- What documents may be checked on arrival
- Whether onward or return proof matters for your case

This is the part you want settled before the last-minute rush.

## Step 3: Your First Few Hours Matter More Than You Think

Many visitors feel their first wave of stress after leaving the airport, not before boarding the flight.  
That is why it helps to decide a few basics in advance:
- How you will get from the airport to the city
- How you will pay for the first few things
- Whether your phone will have power and connectivity
- Whether your hotel address is saved clearly

In many cases, metro or app-based transport is the calmest default choice.

## Step 4: Solve Payment Early, Not on the Spot

In many parts of China, daily spending works most smoothly through mobile payment.  
The tools you are most likely to rely on are:
- Alipay
- WeChat Pay
- QR code payment

Cash is still useful as a backup, but it is often not the smoothest primary method.

## Step 5: Learn the Local Movement Logic

Inside Chinese cities, most visitors quickly end up relying on:
- Metro
- Ride-hailing apps
- Map apps
- Showing screens rather than asking long questions

You do not need to memorize every system in advance, but you do want to get comfortable with the basic operating logic.

## Step 6: For Intercity Travel, Start by Looking at High-Speed Rail

If your route includes multiple cities, high-speed rail is often the first option worth checking.  
Why many travelers like it:
- The process is more predictable
- City-center to city-center travel is often easier
- The overall rhythm can feel calmer than flying

It is not the right answer for every route, but it should usually be part of your first comparison.

## Step 7: Language Is Not the Main Barrier. Workflow Is.

Many first-time visitors overestimate how much depends on speaking Chinese.  
In practice, a lot of daily movement depends more on:
- Phone screens
- Order confirmations
- Map pins
- Translation apps
- Visual confirmation

You do not need perfect language. You need workable tools.

## Final Reminder

Many first-time visitors finish the trip with the same basic reaction:

> “I worried too much before coming.”

China does have its own rhythm and systems, but if you solve the key questions in the right order, the trip usually feels much more manageable than it did in advance.  
Preparation helps. Overthinking usually does not.
